# Clocks, Pressure, and Failure

## Design question

Do time pressure and risk clocks make casework decisions sharper without turning the game into generic meter management?

## Product claim

Grete is running out of time. The player should feel that delay, overreach, and weak evidence all have consequences. But the pressure must stay concrete: clocks should represent case events, not abstract bars.

Core loop for this prototype:

```text
Action takes time → clocks advance → documents/behavior change → decisions become sharper
```

## Scope

Add clocks, timing pressure, and partial failure/success states.

### Includes

- Grete illness clock.
- Rent/economy risk clock.
- Trust clock.
- Optional autonomy/dependency pressure.
- Time advancement after major actions.
- Actions that stabilize one pressure while worsening another.
- Legible failure states.
- Partial success endpoint for the first vertical slice.

## Clock rules

Use Citizen Sleeper-style clocks as pending events, not reversible stats.

- A clock has a concrete name and story question.
- Major actions can fill segments.
- Filled clocks trigger documents, reports, apartment changes, or case events.
- Mitigation usually starts or fills a counter-clock rather than erasing established trouble.
- Every clock movement should be tied to a fact, observation, document, behavior, or consequence.

## Core clocks

### Grete illness

Question:

> How long can Grete keep carrying invisible infrastructure?

Stages:

1. Grete handles everything.
2. Grete tires.
3. Grete forgets/cancels.
4. Grete is hospitalized.
5. Grete is unavailable / dies.

### Economy/rent risk

Question:

> Does the apartment become financially unsafe before supports are in place?

Stages:

1. Stable.
2. Late payment.
3. Warning.
4. Acute risk.
5. Housing crisis.

### Trust

Question:

> Does Elling still allow the case system close enough to help?

Stages:

1. Open.
2. Wary.
3. Defensive.
4. Refusing contact.
5. Case access lost.

### Autonomy/dependency pressure

Question:

> Are supports replacing Grete, or helping Elling carry selected parts himself?

This may not need to be a visible meter. It can be expressed through open questions, consequences, and ending labels.

## Failure states

### Collapse

Too little support.

- Grete dies or is hospitalized.
- Rent/mail/economy chain fails.
- Elling enters crisis placement or loses apartment path.

### Overreach

Too much intrusive support, too fast.

- Elling becomes formally safe but loses agency.
- Trust breaks.
- Case access narrows.
- Heavy services become the only readable option.

### Bureaucratic fake success

Paperwork says solved.

- Vedtak is made.
- Case appears closed.
- Apartment state or Frank report shows the routine never actually took.

### Grete dependency preserved

Player keeps using Grete as the solution.

- Low conflict short-term.
- Grete burden rises.
- Collapse becomes sharper when she disappears.

## Success state

Do not end with a cured or perfectly independent Elling.

Better endpoint:

> Elling can participate enough that the welfare-state investment starts paying back.

Dry civic framing:

> Congratulations. You have created a taxpayer.

Or more Norwegian:

> Skatteetaten er forsiktig optimistisk.

The serious reading:

- Elling keeps the apartment.
- Grete's invisible work is partly transferred.
- Elling gains selected capacities.
- Formal services cover what should not be forced.
- He can give something back.

The humorous-contrast reading:

- All this fragile human work was, officially, a fiscally responsible investment.

## Out of scope

- Full ending tree.
- Multi-year simulation.
- Multiple clients.
- Tax/payment systems.
- Detailed scoring economy.

## Success criteria

- Waiting can be wise or dangerous.
- Grete's decline is felt as design pressure.
- Failure is legible and traceable to choices.
- Humoristisk kontrast survives the serious subject.
- Clocks do not flatten the human situation into generic optimization bars.

## Main risk

Meters flatten the case and make the player optimize instead of understand.

## Mitigation

- Name clocks as pending case events.
- Trigger concrete documents, reports, or apartment changes when clocks fill.
- Avoid reversible abstract meters where possible.
- Keep the civic/taxpayer endpoint dry, not smug.

## Prototype verdict question

After playtesting, ask:

> Did the pressure make the case decisions more interesting, or did it turn Elling into a dashboard?
