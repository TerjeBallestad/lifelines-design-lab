## Motivation

The state-object overlay should move away from DSM / clinical diagnostic language, not away from abstract labels altogether. The useful Cultist Simulator move is to name forces in the room with poetic, emotional, occult-adjacent words — `Shame`, `Affliction`, `Restlessness`, `Contentment` — instead of appointing the client with a diagnosis like anxiety, ADHD, depression, trauma response, or executive dysfunction.

A strong Lifelines image can be both concrete and abstract:

> A pile of unopened bills under the newspaper, with a floating label: `Shame`.

The physical object is the evidence. The floating label is the pressure/force. Together they are stronger than either alone.

## Scope

Revise the state-object model and UI language so every state object combines:

- a **non-clinical condition label** — poetic / social / emotional / situational, not diagnostic;
- a **physical anchor** in the room or on the case desk;
- visible signs that let the player read why this pressure is present;
- mechanical consequences: what it blocks, worsens, attracts, softens, or transforms into.

Do not force all UI to lead with mundane object names. The point is the pairing: physical evidence + atmospheric condition label + mechanical pressure.

## Acceptance

- State labels avoid DSM / clinical diagnostic terms and do not assign mental illnesses to clients.
- Labels may be abstract/emotional when they feel Cultist-like: `Shame`, `Affliction`, `Restlessness`, `Silence`, `Relief`, `Obligation`, etc.
- Each abstract label has a concrete room/case-desk anchor, e.g. `Shame` attached to unopened bills under the newspaper.
- UI makes the anchor-label relationship visible: the player sees what object or routine the pressure clings to.
- At least five pressure/state objects are rewritten to use this pattern.
- Frank/case-file interpretation remains cautious: it reads pressures and situations, not diagnoses.

## Anti-goals

- No `Anxiety`, `Depression`, `ADHD`, `trauma response`, `executive dysfunction`, or similar clinical/diagnostic labels as player-facing state objects.
- No rule that abstract labels are bad by default.
- No purely abstract floating mental-state tags without a visible room/case anchor.
